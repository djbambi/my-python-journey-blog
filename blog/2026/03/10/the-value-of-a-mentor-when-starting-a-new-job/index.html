    The value of a mentor when starting a new job

    Starting a new technical role always comes with a certain amount of uncertainty.

    New systems.
    New people.
    New expectations.

    Even when you know you’re capable, there’s usually a period where it feels like you’re operating slightly outside your comfort zone.

    For me, the early weeks of my new role had exactly that feeling.

    What made a huge difference was having a mentor to talk things through with.

    The early phase: imposter syndrome

    The first few days in a new job can be strange.

    You suddenly find yourself surrounded by:

    • unfamiliar tools
    • unfamiliar codebases
    • unfamiliar architecture

    Even if you’ve done similar work before, there’s an inevitable moment where you think:

    “Maybe I’m out of my depth here.”

    Logically, you know that’s unlikely.
    But emotionally, it’s still easy to feel that way when everything around you is new.

    Having a mentor during that phase helped enormously.

    Not because they solved the problems for me, but because they helped normalise the experience.

    The message was simple:

    Everyone goes through this phase when starting somewhere new.

    Just hearing that from someone experienced takes a surprising amount of pressure away.

    A sounding board for ideas

    One of the most valuable parts of mentoring isn’t instruction, it’s conversation.

    Being able to talk through problems, ideas, or uncertainties with someone outside your immediate work environment changes the dynamic completely.

    Instead of thinking:

    “I need to figure this out perfectly before I say anything.”

    You can say:

    “Here’s what I’m seeing. Does this make sense?”

    That small shift makes it much easier to reason through technical or architectural questions.

    Often the answer emerges just from explaining the situation clearly.

    A practical framework for working

    Another big benefit was having a clearer framework for how to approach development work.

    Through the PyBites PDM programme I was introduced to ideas such as:

    • structuring Python projects properly
    • using modern tooling like uv, ruff, and pre-commit
    • working with pull requests and clean development workflows
    • thinking deliberately about project structure

    None of these things are individually revolutionary.

    But together they create a way of working.

    That framework gave me a solid starting point when tackling problems at work, rather than feeling like I was improvising everything from scratch.

    Confidence to contribute

    One interesting side effect of that framework was increased confidence.

    Because I had exposure to practices such as:

    • automated testing
    • code quality checks
    • pre-commit hooks
    • structured project layouts

    I felt comfortable suggesting ideas within my team that hadn’t been widely adopted yet.

    Not because I was certain they were perfect solutions, but because I had enough understanding to contribute to the discussion.

    That’s a subtle but important shift.

    Instead of feeling like the new person trying to catch up, you start feeling like someone who can add value.

    Architectural thinking

    Another unexpected benefit was exposure to higher-level design ideas.

    Concepts such as:

    • repository patterns
    • abstract base classes
    • clearer separation of responsibilities

    started to change how I looked at problems.

    Sometimes you recognise patterns that you previously would have solved in a much more ad-hoc way.

    Other times you simply become more deliberate about design decisions.

    Either way, it pushes you toward writing code that is easier to understand and maintain.

    The real value: a roadmap

    If I had to summarise the biggest benefit of mentoring in one phrase, it would be this:

    It gave me a roadmap.

    Not a rigid set of instructions, but a sense of direction.

    Instead of trying to learn everything randomly, there was a clearer idea of:

    • what good engineering practices look like
    • which tools are worth investing in
    • how experienced developers approach problems

    That makes the early weeks of a new role much easier to navigate.

    Final reflection

    A mentor doesn’t remove the need to do the work yourself.

    You still have to:

    • learn the systems
    • investigate problems
    • write the code
    • make the mistakes

    But having someone experienced to talk things through with can accelerate that process dramatically.

    It reduces uncertainty, reinforces good practices, and helps you build confidence faster.

    For me, it felt a bit like having a map at the start of a journey.

    You still have to walk the path, but you’re far less likely to wander in circles.
